About

Security research that takes the human side seriously

Program PhD in Computer Science · Colorado State University, USA
Prior B.Sc. CSE · Chittagong University of Engineering and Technology (CUET), Bangladesh

I am a PhD student in the Department of Computer Science at Colorado State University, working with Dr. McKenna McCall. My research is about the gap between what security systems can guarantee on paper and what people are actually willing to trust and deploy. Closing that gap takes more than better engineering.

On the IFC side, I am building empirical foundations for understanding where real information flow vulnerabilities live in production software. The other project studies trusted execution environments and asks a practical question: what makes them technically promising, and what still makes organizations hesitant to trust them?

Alongside security research, I have also worked on the application of Machine Learning, and AR/VR studies. That mix continues to shape how I approach evidence, reproducibility, and research writing. Before coming to CSU, I spent about two years as an instructor at Green University of Bangladesh.

Research Focus

Current directions and broader interests

CURRENT · PROJECT 01

Information Flow Control & Vulnerability Analysis

Most IFC tools get abandoned because they ask too much from developers. This project starts from the other end: looking at real CVE data to understand what kinds of information flow problems actually show up in production software, and whether the controls developers already have access to would have caught them. The work involves building filtering pipelines, clustering vulnerability text at scale, and careful manual annotation to separate signal from noise.

CURRENT · PROJECT 02

Trusted Execution Environments in Practice

TEEs sound compelling on paper. Adopting Intel SGX or similar hardware enclaves for sensitive workloads in healthcare involves considerably more than getting the engineering right. This project examines the compliance burden, legal exposure, vendor accountability, and whether institutions can realistically trust the attestation story when patient data or AI model weights are running inside an enclave they cannot fully inspect.

BROADER INTERESTS

Usable Security, HCI, and Interactive Systems

I am broadly interested in how security systems, interactive tools, and immersive environments are evaluated, explained, and made usable for real people.

Research Projects

Recent research writing, and graduate course projects

Spring 2026 · CS 540

Can AI Reduce Warning Fatigue?

A structured literature review on learning-based approaches for static-analysis security alerts, covering warning prioritization, explanation and usability support, automated repair, and hybrid end-to-end pipelines.

Fall 2025

SoK: Usable Security Type Systems

A systematization of developer-oriented usability evidence for security type systems, focused on evaluation methods, developer effort, feedback quality, and practical design features.

Fall 2025

CVSS v3.x vs. v4.0 Scoring Analysis

A large-scale study of 8,133 paired CVEs that examines score shifts, severity migration, and the metric-level reasons vulnerabilities move across severity bands.

Spring 2025 · CS 535

Scalable Multi-Document Text Summarization

A distributed deep learning project using Multi-News, PEGASUS, BART, and LED, with PyTorch Distributed Data Parallel and Apache Spark for scalable training and evaluation.

Spring 2025 · CS 465

LayeredScreen: Depth-Aware Window Switching for XR

An XR interaction study on HoloLens 2 that compared layered window-management techniques and showed strong user preference for simpler, familiar layouts.

Fall 2024 · CS 464

Stress Relievers: VR-Based Stress Management

A virtual reality study built in Unity HDRP on Meta Quest 3, using beach, forest, and campfire environments and heart-rate-based measurement to evaluate stress reduction.
